# Worldbuilding Desk Market Research Brief

I am building a desktop writing app for fiction authors called **Worldbuilding Desk**.

The core idea is:

- make the writing workspace the primary surface
- let canon, worldbuilding, character state, and continuity support appear progressively
- keep authors in flow instead of forcing them into database setup or planning rituals before they can draft

This is **not** meant to be a generic AI writing app, and it is **not** meant to be only a wiki/world-bible tool.

The intended product position is:

- a writing-first workspace for novelists, LitRPG authors, progression fantasy authors, and system-heavy fiction writers
- with passive continuity support, structured lore support, and eventually deterministic state validation

## Current Product Shape

Today, the app already has:

- a writing workspace with scenes/documents
- a World Bible with dynamic categories and fields
- character records and character sheets
- import/export flows
- review flows for unknown names, ambiguous references, and canon contradictions
- scratchpad and corkboard planning surfaces
- AI assistance for author-invoked help
- a local-review architecture where deterministic validation is the source of truth and local AI can annotate review issues

The long-term differentiator is **not** text generation. It is:

- keeping story context coherent while authors draft
- making lore and state tracking useful without heavy manual upkeep
- eventually tracking scene-derived state changes across chapters with a deterministic ledger/replay model

## Product Thesis To Stress-Test

The thesis is:

> Authors want a writing-first environment that helps maintain canon and story-state continuity without turning the drafting process into project management.

Corollary:

- worldbuilding tools are useful, but many feel like separate databases detached from writing
- AI writing tools are useful, but many feel generic and do not manage continuity well
- system-heavy fiction authors may especially value deterministic support for state, inventory, progression, resources, and lore consistency

## Research Request

Please do a deep market and product landscape analysis for this product direction.

I want a serious answer, not encouragement.

## Questions To Answer

### 1. Market Landscape

- What categories does this product sit between?
  - writing apps
  - worldbuilding tools
  - AI writing tools
  - story planning tools
  - TTRPG/LitRPG/system-tracking tools
- Are there existing products converging on this same space?
- Is this a real category gap or just an awkward overlap between existing categories?

### 2. Competitive Analysis

Please compare this concept against tools such as:

- Scrivener
- Obsidian-based writing/worldbuilding workflows
- Campfire
- Plottr
- World Anvil
- Novelcrafter
- Sudowrite
- Lore Forge / Fantasia Archive / similar worldbuilding tools
- any newer AI-native writing tools or continuity tools

For each, I want to know:

- what job it does best
- where it is weak
- whether it threatens this product directly or only partially
- what user behavior it trains that would make switching hard

### 3. User Segments

Who is the best fit for this product?

Please segment likely users, for example:

- general novelists
- fantasy authors
- LitRPG / progression fantasy authors
- serialized web fiction writers
- game writers / narrative designers
- heavy planners vs discovery writers

For each segment, assess:

- pain level
- willingness to adopt a new tool
- willingness to pay
- likely objections
- whether deterministic continuity/state support is actually valuable or mostly theoretical

### 4. Product Differentiation

What is the strongest wedge?

Evaluate these possible wedges:

- writing-first + passive canon review
- local-first/private background review
- deterministic story-state validation
- World Bible integrated directly into drafting
- support for system-heavy fiction and progression mechanics

Which wedge is strongest?
Which wedge is easiest to explain?
Which wedge sounds impressive but is actually weak?

### 5. AI Strategy

Please assess whether the dual-model/product strategy makes sense:

- small/background/local model for passive review and structured extraction
- separate author-controlled model for brainstorming, critique, prose help, and explicit assistant interactions

Questions:

- Is this a real product advantage or just architectural neatness?
- Will users understand the difference?
- Is “background AI for continuity/state review” actually valuable enough to justify the complexity?
- What is the likely user perception of local/private background AI versus hosted creative AI?

### 6. Risk Analysis

What are the biggest product risks?

Please evaluate risks such as:

- becoming too complicated
- trying to serve too many adjacent jobs
- building impressive architecture before proving user demand
- continuity/state enforcement sounding more useful than it is in practice
- writers resisting any system that feels managerial or interruptive
- worldbuilding features pulling attention away from the writing-first promise

I want brutal honesty here.

### 7. Go-To-Market and Messaging

What should the product **not** say?

Please help identify messaging that would weaken positioning, for example:

- “AI writing assistant”
- “all-in-one authoring IDE”
- “worldbuilding database”
- “project management for writers”

Then propose:

- a clearer product category
- a one-sentence positioning statement
- a few landing-page headline directions
- which audiences should be targeted first

### 8. Strategic Recommendation

At the end, please answer directly:

- Is this a promising product direction?
- For whom?
- Why now?
- What should be simplified or narrowed?
- What should be deprioritized?
- What is the most credible first wedge?
- What would make this fail even if execution is competent?
